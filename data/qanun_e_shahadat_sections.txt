=== Article 1: Short Title and Commencement ===
This Article provides the formal introduction to the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984. It states that the law shall be known by this title and extends to the whole of Pakistan, thereby giving it nationwide applicability. It further declares that the Order shall come into force at once, meaning from the date of its promulgation it becomes operative without requiring further notification. This Article establishes the authority, scope, and enforceability of the law governing evidence. It serves as the foundational clause ensuring that all subsequent provisions relating to admissibility, relevancy, and proof of facts apply uniformly across all courts within the jurisdiction of Pakistan.

=== Article 2: Definitions ===
This Article defines key terms essential for interpreting the law of evidence. A “fact” includes anything capable of being perceived by senses or any mental condition of which a person is conscious. A “relevant fact” is one connected to another fact in a manner recognized by the Order. “Document” includes any matter expressed on any substance by means of letters, figures, or marks intended for recording information. “Evidence” includes oral statements made before the Court and documents produced for inspection. The terms “proved,” “disproved,” and “not proved” describe degrees of judicial satisfaction. A fact is proved when the Court believes it exists or considers it highly probable; disproved when it believes it does not exist; and not proved when neither conclusion is reached. These definitions form the backbone of evidentiary evaluation.

=== Article 17: Proof of Facts by Oral Evidence ===
This Article lays down that all facts, except the contents of documents, may be proved by oral evidence. Oral evidence refers to statements made by witnesses before the Court regarding facts they have personally seen, heard, or perceived. The principle emphasizes direct evidence, requiring that witnesses testify to facts within their own knowledge rather than hearsay. Where documentary evidence exists, its contents must be proved by producing the document itself, subject to exceptions. The Article ensures reliability by prioritizing firsthand testimony and limiting reliance on indirect or secondhand information. It forms a core rule distinguishing between oral and documentary proof.

=== Article 71: Proof of Signature and Handwriting ===
Under this Article, when a document is alleged to be signed or written by a particular person, the signature or handwriting must be proved to be that of such person. Proof may be established through direct evidence, expert opinion, comparison by the Court, or testimony of a person acquainted with the handwriting. The burden lies on the party asserting authenticity. This provision ensures that documents relied upon in legal proceedings are genuine and attributable to the person concerned. It prevents fraud and forgery by requiring verification before a document can be accepted as evidence.

=== Article 117: Burden of Proof ===
This Article establishes the fundamental rule that the burden of proof lies on the person who asserts the existence of a fact and seeks a legal judgment based on it. The party must prove facts that support his claim or defence. If he fails to do so, the Court will decide against him. This principle ensures fairness by requiring each party to substantiate their assertions rather than relying on the weakness of the opponent’s case. It forms the basis of evidentiary responsibility in both civil and criminal proceedings.

=== Article 118: On Whom Burden of Proof Lies ===
This provision clarifies that the burden of proof lies on the party who would fail if no evidence were presented by either side. It determines initial responsibility in litigation. For example, in civil cases, the plaintiff typically bears the burden because he seeks relief. However, the burden may shift during proceedings depending on evidence presented. The Article provides a practical test to identify which party must begin proving facts. It ensures orderly conduct of trials and prevents ambiguity regarding evidentiary obligations.

=== Article 119: Burden of Proof as to Particular Fact ===
This Article states that the burden of proof for any specific fact lies on the person who wishes the Court to believe in its existence, unless the law provides otherwise. It operates alongside general burden rules and applies to individual facts within a case. For instance, if a party claims a particular exception or special circumstance, he must prove it. The Article allows allocation of proof responsibility for distinct issues within the same case, ensuring that each factual assertion is properly supported by evidence.

=== Article 121: Burden of Proving Fact Especially Within Knowledge ===
Where a fact is especially within the knowledge of a particular person, the burden of proving that fact lies upon him. This rule is based on practicality and fairness, as it would be difficult for the opposing party to prove facts not accessible to them. For example, matters relating to a person’s intention, internal records, or personal conduct may fall within this category. The Article shifts evidentiary responsibility to the party best positioned to produce proof, ensuring efficient administration of justice.

=== Article 129: Estoppel ===
This Article embodies the doctrine of estoppel, which prevents a person from denying a fact that he has previously represented as true and upon which another person has relied. If one party, by declaration, act, or omission, causes another to believe something and act upon it, he cannot later contradict that representation in legal proceedings. The principle promotes fairness, consistency, and good faith in dealings. Estoppel operates as a rule of evidence, barring contradictory claims and protecting parties from injustice arising from reliance on representations.

=== Article 150: Who May Testify ===
This Article declares that all persons are competent to testify unless the Court considers them incapable of understanding questions or giving rational answers. Incapacity may arise due to tender age, old age, disease, or mental or physical disability. Competency is thus a question of ability rather than status. Even children may testify if they possess sufficient understanding. The Court evaluates competency before recording evidence. The provision ensures inclusivity while maintaining reliability of testimony.

=== Article 151: Dumb Witnesses ===
A witness who is unable to speak may give evidence in any intelligible manner, such as writing or signs. Such communication must be made in open Court to ensure transparency and authenticity. The evidence so given is treated as oral evidence. The Court may take assistance of interpreters if necessary. This Article ensures that disability does not prevent a person from giving testimony, thereby promoting access to justice while maintaining procedural safeguards.

=== Article 152: Parties to Civil Suit and Their Spouses — Competency ===
In civil proceedings, the parties to the suit, as well as their husbands or wives, are competent witnesses. This provision removes earlier restrictions on spousal testimony and allows relevant evidence to be presented. Competency does not mean compellability in all cases, but it ensures that such persons are not disqualified merely due to their relationship. The Article enhances the availability of evidence and supports fair adjudication by permitting those closely connected to the dispute to testify where appropriate.
